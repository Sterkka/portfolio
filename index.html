Exploring the Process of Mobile Reporting By Blue-Collar Mobile Workers A user research case (2018-2019)
System

Mobile application and desktop application

Work done

User research, usability evaluation, improvement suggestion, Master's Thesis

Synopsis

As part of my thesis I investigated the use of a mobile reporting solution by construction workers, drivers and maintainers who work for the city of Tampere. The primary objective was to learn what kind of contextual limitations and usability issues affect the process of reporting data from the field.
Running a contextual inquiry out in the field

The project ran in cooperation with Tampereen Infra, which granted access to their workers. I first carried out a field study in different mobile work settings, interviewed and observed workers carrying out real work tasks and interacting with devices. I reported my findings to my partners Protieto throughout the duration of the project.

To immerse myself in the day-to-day jobs of blue-collar mobile workers I was assisted by the contextual inquiry user-centered research method. My main objective was to learn about practices of using different mobile devices to report job-related information with focus put on the usability of one mobile reporting application.

Mobile work and mobile technology

Mobile workers hold higher levels of accountability to report information about their work tasks nowadays. Their job has distinctive technical characteristics which subsequently affect their ability to answer the demand to use technology in the field.

My visits to the field documented how workers improvise by delaying data entry, by using alternative technologies to report and by mainly prioritizing their physical work task. This leads to interesting reporting habits, which were reported and analyzed in my Master's thesis.

Link to my thesis

The benefit to the company

The company was able to learn how their product is used in different contexts and to get acquainted with various usage scenarios and reporting practices that take place out in mobile work environments. They were also able to pinpoint the positive and the negative aspects of their product.

Further, they benefited from receiving user insight and future process improvement considerations.

E-learning Platform For Teachers Service and conceptual design case (2017)
Platform

Website

Work done

User research, interactive prototyping, user testing

Synopsis

The Ministry of Education and Culture's OpenDigi project aims to implement a platform that will help develop the professional competence of teachers. Our task was to conceptualize a digital learning experience and demonstrate it with a prototype.
Great LX starts from understanding the teacher

We've reached out to teachers via forums and social media and they were happy to help us by joining an online group that we've created for discussion and exchange of ideas.

By hearing what teachers had to say we then were able to draw their needs. We've used other methods such online surveys and phone interviews to gain more understading. At the same time, we've conducted a competitor analysis to assess the strenght and weaknesses of other e-learning platforms on the market.

Gathering was only the first step, next up, ideation

By analyzing and ideating we've learned that the main challenge for the creation of an e-learning platform is supporting individualistic learning paths of teachers from different backgrounds and skill-sets.

After collecting the data, personas were created based on what was learned. During the ideation stage we've envisioned four intriguing concepts to test out with users.

Coneptualizing is part of the deal

Earlier, we drew out the outlines for our four different e-learning concepts. Each concept represented a different approach towards e-learning.

The results of the ideation phase were concretized by the creation of wireframes. These were later revamped into slightly higher fidelity mockups that were tested by teachers both online and face-to-face.

Interactive prototype

Subsequently, after we've gathered enough feedback and insight about our initial concepts, the final concept was selected and was ready to be validated. The validation process yielded more ideas for improvements that were finally presented to the client.

Need Laughs? We Got 'Em! Booking Entertainment Redesign of a webpage in a human-centered design approach (2017)
Platform

Website

Work done

User research, prototyping, user testing

Synopsis

Naurunpaikka is an entertainment booking agency that approached human-technology interaction students of the University of Tampere with the challenge of revamping their rather outdated website.
GUI design starts by engaging the user

The first step was the conduct of a competitive analysis to learn what could the competition tell us about the entertainment business. We've learned that the process of booking entertainment should be aided by a simple and consistent design, provided with useful information and testimonials and also includes advance search features and straight-forward navigation.

But our human-centered design process would not have been succesful without conducting interviews with potential users. We did our best to get to know the users and whay they need. By this we understood what kind of features are more desirable.

Ideating to build castles in the sky and then the bridges

After the user research phase we pursued with the ideation process, which was creative, insightful and most importantly entertaining. This process felt incredibly fruitful since our team did brainstormed a lot, but parts of the work were kept individualy. Our team did their best to enjoy the process, which was reflected in the representations of our design thinking stage.

We created affinity diagrams, sequence models and playful personas and ended up with the broadest range of possibilities that we could. Afterwards, we turned into creating wireframes, with a clear idea for some parts of our design in mind. Other aspects of our design, such as the digital shop, were addressed and sketched last.

From a rough skeleton to a visual prototype

Along the way we've tested out our ideas by rapid means such as guerrilla prototyping and a low-fidellity prototype until we saw our clickable prototype rises from those rough & dirty sketches.
This process was made possible by contacting classmates, friends and colleagues. Nevertheless, we received great feedback from everywhere around. Any constructive feedback we got has helped to shape our last prototype.

Finally, we presented our outcome in front of the class with our client from Naurunpaikka present. The first response that we received was that "It's the best comedian site in the wörld!"

Guided By the Light: Light Guided Way Finding System An interaction design case (2017)
System

Custom Rasberry Pi demo + custom UI

Work done

Interaction design, Service design

Synopsis

As part of the HTI university course we were asked to innovate by using modern technology available at our disposal. This led us to explore the restaurant industry and to design an interactive solution that guides restaurant customers to their dining tables.
Researching restaurant usage scenarios

After researching different usage scenarios that occur upon visiting restaurants we've noticed that the procedure of locating a table isn't always so straight-forward for customers. Further, it was discovered that restaurant staff members are often too busy to instruct arriving customers to vacant seats.

Using cheap technology to solve the problem

We found a cheap yet sophisticated way to recognize the amount of entering customers, to direct them to a preferred seat by solely interacting with a touchscreen and to alert the staff upon table occupancy.

Technology used:

  • Custom made prototype UI for user interaction.
  • Microsoft Kinect for recognizing and counting diners.
  • A prototype light guide case built from a Rasberry Pi, LED light and a simple USB camera.
Testing

At the end of the project we ran two different experiments to test our system. We converted a learning space within our faculty into a restaurant environment, and we hooked up and mounted the prototype.

In the first test we were evaluating the system's GUI, the cognitive load and validated parameters with different group sizes. We found out that the GUI should require more explicit text and animations and less active confirmations from the users. We also concluded that we should reduce the screen size to minimize cognitive load and better diffuze the LED light.

In the second test we further investigated mapping and representation of information in the GUI. We measured how succesful is the system in aiding people in locating a vacant table and used both control and experimental groups to do so.

A Municipal Digital Experience Service Design (2016)
Platform

Mobile app

Work done

User research, benchmarking, prototyping

Synopsis

The municipality of Ylöjärvi wanted us to develop a mobile application concept that will provide citizens a great municipal digital experience. We rose to the challenge and came up with a concept that aims to increase participation, e.g. by voting, reporting hazards and by receiving services and information.

A consolidated town that strives for a sense of a community

We headed over to Ylöjärvi to inquire about the sentiment of local citizens. After attending a discussion in the town hall we chatted with local politicians and other political and social players. We learned that the newly consolidated parts of town, which are located quite far from the center, have somewhat lower levels of accessibility to municipal services.

In the central part of town we asked locals to tell about their daily municipal life experiences and heard that despite the pleasant life, they lack a sense of cohesiveness. This is mostly due to the geographical dispersement of residents, lack of hang-out spots and low quality of local services.

To mobile or not to mobile?

We also published an online survey to understand the ups and downs of local life, especially in regards to the quality of municipal services. We aimed at understading the attitudes of residents towards mobile technology and other digital services. We wanted to learn whether or not a mobile application can constitute a viable platform for civilians and city officials alike.

The survey results have indicated that there is readiness for digitalizaion within the community. This in light of the inequality in access to municipal services and positive opinions about digitalization of local services. Further, social media has already played a part in bringing people together as it was a highly popular mean for discussion and exchange of information and goods.

The outcome

As we developed our concept the emphasis was put on bridging geographical gaps by bringing residents together. The digital prototype aimed at exemplifying how inhabitants can take part in decision making processes by answering polls and surveys, by initiating ideas and events and by reporting faults. Our prototype also incorporated a gamified feature to induce participation and motivate people to use the application.

The final prototype was used to demonstrate to decision makers the potential of a municipal digital experience in catering information and provide access to services digitally. The end result has received positive feedback from our partners and has also been licensed.

The team and my part in it

I had the pleasure to work with an outstanding team consisted of students from different backgrounds and nationalities. Some team members have since then returned to their home countries and we remained in touch until this day.

As part of my responsibilities in the project I was:

  • Conducting a competitor analysis
  • Leading the user research
  • Phrasing surveys and analyzing collected data
  • Digital prototyping
  • Taking an active role in partner meetings, preparing presentations and presenting
  • Conceptualizing a dashboard for city employees

University library online database An usability evaluation (2015)
Platform

Website

Work done

Heuristic evaluation, Usability lab evaluation

Synopsis

The universities library had woken up to the fact that their systems were grossly outdated. Their new beta was already in testing and our job was to identify usability issues with the new system.
Everything started with a heuristic evalution

To understand were the potential issues might exist, we started with an expert evalution in the form of a heuristic evaluation. We pinpointed several issues, some major ones and some less severe.

Finally we tested with users

To provide support for our findings and find more issues that might have gone unnoticed, we organized usability testing in a lab. User were given tasks and their progress was recorded and analyzed. Multiple issues were confirmed and potential fixes were communicated to the client.
